A BIT ABOUT
SOIL BENCHMARK

First a bit of context on who we are and how we ended up building this.

HOW WE STARTED

Founded in 2022 by Tom Scrope and I

A vague idea that we wanted to do something for soils at scale — no real clue what.

We founded Soil Benchmark in 2022. I'd been a soil scientist at the James Hutton Institute in Aberdeen for five years. Tom had been working in the biofertiliser side of a company called NovaQ. We had a vague idea of doing something to improve soils at scale — but it really wasn't clear what that something was.

EIGHT MONTHS OF WANDERING

Lots of ideas. Nothing that quite stuck.

We spent roughly 8 months trying to figure out what to actually build.

Then a new piece of UK government policy landed — and suddenly there was an ideal soil-related problem for us to solve.

For about 8 months we were just trying things, talking to farmers, talking to advisors. Lots of half-ideas. The thing that finally gave us a focus was the introduction of the Sustainable Farming Incentive in England.

ENTER SAM1

The Sustainable Farming Incentive action that kicked things off

💷
£6 / hectare
Paid to farms with an up-to-date soil management plan and soil organic matter results.
📋
+ £95 / year
Flat annual payment, on top.

Suddenly there was a national-scale incentive to produce soil management plans — and the price-per-hectare was tight enough that it had to be fast to be worth doing.

gov.uk/guidance/how-to-do-the-sfi-actions-for-soils#how-to-assess-soil-produce-a-soil-management-plan-and-test-soil-organic-matter-sam1

SAM1 is the SFI action for assessing soil, producing a soil management plan and testing soil organic matter. Defra pays £6 per hectare plus a £95 flat annual payment. £6/ha sounds small, but at scale it's meaningful — the catch is that without technology, paying a consultant to do this would eat the payment several times over.

TWO REALISATIONS

After reading the soil management plan requirements

⏳
Doing this manually would be brutally time-consuming — £6/ha wouldn't cover a consultant.
🗺️
But almost every map layer needed to do it digitally already existed.

So — we built a system to use that data and make the process quick enough to be worth doing.

Reading through the requirements for a soil management plan, two things became obvious. First, the job would be incredibly time-consuming without technology. Second, almost all the geospatial map layers required to do the job digitally already existed in the UK. Those two together is what convinced us to build.

Part 2

THE DATA BEHIND
OUR DESK BASED SOIL ASSESSMENT

Six layers do most of the work

The system stands on a handful of geospatial datasets. Let's walk through each.

TOPOGRAPHY

EA national LIDAR composite — the foundation of the whole system

📐
Elevation for every square metre of England
🍀
Released spring 2023 — just as we started
📦
A few hundred GB — we literally posted a hard drive to the EA
🌱
So many derived layers come from this one source

environment.data.gov.uk/dataset/13787b9a-26a4-4775-8523-806d13af58fc · Open Government Licence

Topography is the bedrock. We got incredibly lucky — in spring 2023, just as we were starting to build, the Environment Agency finished their national LIDAR survey: 1m resolution elevation across the whole of England, released under Open Government Licence. The dataset is huge — a few hundred GB — so we posted a hard drive to the EA and waited a couple of months for them to upload it and post it back. From this single dataset you derive slope, aspect, flow accumulation, hill-shading — an enormous amount of what the system needs.

SOIL

Estimating topsoil texture across the country

🪨
BGS Soil Parent Material model — what we use today
🆕
LandIS portal now has the National Soil Map of England open-access — one for v2

bgs.ac.uk/datasets/soil-parent-material-model · portal.landis.org.uk

For soil we needed something that gave us topsoil texture nationally. We chose the BGS Soil Parent Material model. More recently the LandIS portal has made the National Soil Map of England and other layers open access — so a future version of the system will lean on that.

WATER

Down to the field-ditch level

💧
Ordnance Survey NGD water dataset
🚜
Crucial for the proximity-to-watercourse rules in the plan
✅
Has worked well for us for the last few years
A soil management plan needs you to define how close a field is to a waterway, including small features like field ditches. The Ordnance Survey NGD water dataset gives us that level of detail and it's worked really well for us over the past few years.

BOREHOLES

Pollution-risk proxies for groundwater

🕳️
BGS Single Onshore Boreholes Index
⚠️
Fields near boreholes = potential to pollute water sources

bgs.ac.uk/datasets/boreholes-index

Boreholes matter because fields near them represent a route to polluting water sources. The BGS Single Onshore Boreholes Index gives us a national picture of where they are.

SENSITIVE AREAS

NVZ, SSSI, SAC … and a geospatial gotcha

🛡️
NVZ, SSSI, SAC and similar designations
📏
Edge cases: NVZ boundaries that nip a tiny corner of a field aren't really “in” the sensitive area
Defining whether a field is in some sort of sensitive area — an NVZ, SSSI, SAC — is required by the plan. There's a slight gotcha: these polygons are usually drawn along field boundaries, but you'll get tiny edge intersections that don't actually represent the field being inside the sensitive area. We have to handle that explicitly so we don't flag fields that aren't really affected.

SLOPE DIRECTION ARROWS

The unsung hero of map interpretation

↘️
Tiny arrows showing which way water actually flows
👀
Reading a slope map without them is way harder than it sounds
Worth calling out — slope direction arrows derived from the LIDAR DTM. They're a small thing but they make the difference between a map being abstract and a map being immediately readable when you're trying to interpret runoff or pollution risk.

Walk through the logic: we pull the slope class from the run-off risk layer, join it with BGS soil texture, collapse texture into four broad categories (Sandy/light, Medium, Calcareous, Heavy), then look up the erosion-risk class on a texture × slope grid. The output is a four-level ordered factor: Low → Moderate → High → Very High.

On top of the four risk layers we run a handful of proximity-based checks on each field: within 20 m of a watercourse, within 50 m of a borehole, or intersecting a sensitive area like an NVZ, SSSI or SAC. These flags feed directly into the soil management plan recommendations.

SPREADING MAPS

Where it's safe to apply slurry, manure or fertiliser — and where it isn't

Built from four input layers…

Output: a four-class map — green, yellow, orange, red.

Spreading maps are one of the headline outputs of the system. We combine topography, soil, boreholes and water data into a single per-field classification: spread freely, use carefully, avoid, or don't spread at all. The red “do not spread” zones are typically along watercourses and around boreholes — you can see the buffers around the borehole and the river in this example.

Three colour bands: red is hard exclusions — buffers around water, springs, boreholes, plus Ramsar. Orange is avoid-in-winter — very steep slopes, slope-and-water combinations, and SSSI. Yellow is restrict-in-winter — slope × soil × water-adjacency combinations, shallow soils, and any field with drains. Anything not flagged stays as the green default.
